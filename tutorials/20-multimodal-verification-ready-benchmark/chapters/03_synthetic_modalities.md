# Synthetic modalities

Several observations are rendered from one hidden architecture. The SEM-like image emphasizes relief and boundary contrast. The fluorescence-like image has blur and illumination variation. The polarization-like modality carries intensity and direct orientation information. The DIC texture supports kinematic observation.

The point is complementarity. One modality may be better for boundaries, another for orientation, another for deformation. Combining them is meaningful only because their shared ground truth is known.

The images are intentionally simplified but reproducible. They are designed for controlled verification, not for claiming experimental realism.

The chapter should be read together with the code. Every quantity mentioned here is created explicitly in `multimodal_benchmark.py` and regenerated by `reproduce.py`. The purpose is not to hide complexity behind a black box, but to show which assumptions enter the pipeline.

For interpretation, always ask four questions: what is the hidden truth, what is observed, what is reconstructed, and which metric compares them? This four-part logic prevents a visually appealing result from being mistaken for a verified mechanical conclusion.

## Implementation details

The SEM-like modality is derived from a smoothed height proxy and edge contrast. The polarization-like modality stores both intensity and noisy orientation. The fluorescence-like modality includes blur and uneven illumination. The DIC image is generated as a separate speckle-like texture. These choices create a controlled mismatch between what is best for morphology, what is best for orientation and what is best for kinematics.

## What to check while reading

Compare `synthetic_modalities.png` with `ground_truth_fields.png`. Notice that no single modality shows everything perfectly. Boundaries, pores, orientation and deformation texture are distributed across different observations. This is the reason the final benchmark is multimodal: it asks whether complementary observations help the complete mechanical inference task.

## Typical misunderstanding

It is tempting to ask which modality is “best” in general. That question is incomplete. A modality can be best for segmentation and weak for orientation, or good for orientation and weak for boundaries. The correct question is: best for which hidden quantity and for which downstream decision?
