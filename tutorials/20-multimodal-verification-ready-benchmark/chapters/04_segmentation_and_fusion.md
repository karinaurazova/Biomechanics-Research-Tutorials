# Segmentation and fusion

The segmentation stage includes global thresholding, Otsu thresholding, adaptive thresholding and a simple multimodal fusion score. These baselines are transparent so that students can understand the assumptions before moving to learned models, SAM prompts or μSAM workflows.

The metrics include Dice, IoU, precision, recall, area error and continuity. Dice alone is insufficient because a thin broken bridge can barely change pixel overlap while strongly changing mechanical load paths.

Fusion is implemented as a weighted combination of modalities. It is not optimal; it is a reference point for future comparisons with more advanced methods.

The chapter should be read together with the code. Every quantity mentioned here is created explicitly in `multimodal_benchmark.py` and regenerated by `reproduce.py`. The purpose is not to hide complexity behind a black box, but to show which assumptions enter the pipeline.

For interpretation, always ask four questions: what is the hidden truth, what is observed, what is reconstructed, and which metric compares them? This four-part logic prevents a visually appealing result from being mistaken for a verified mechanical conclusion.

## Implementation details

The segmentation functions intentionally avoid heavy dependencies. Otsu thresholding is implemented directly, adaptive thresholding uses a local Gaussian mean, and morphology removes isolated noise. The fusion mask combines normalized SEM-like, fluorescence-like and polarization-like intensities. Because the methods are simple, students can trace why a mask succeeds or fails.

## What to check while reading

Open `segmentation_metrics.csv` and compare Dice with continuity. Then open `segmentation_results.png`. Look for cases where the mask looks acceptable but changes connectivity or fills pores. These are precisely the failures that matter for image-informed mechanics. A small topological error can become a large error in anisotropic stiffness or inverse parameters.

## Typical misunderstanding

A high Dice score is not a guarantee of mechanical usefulness. Dice is dominated by area overlap. It does not know whether a load-bearing bridge was broken, whether a pore was filled, or whether the remaining mask supports a correct orientation estimate. This is why the benchmark carries segmentation outputs into the mechanical stage instead of stopping at pixel metrics.
