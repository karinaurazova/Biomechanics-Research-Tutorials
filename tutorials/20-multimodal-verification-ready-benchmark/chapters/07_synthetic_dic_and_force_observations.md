# Synthetic DIC and force observations

DIC-like strain fields are generated as exact fields with controlled noise. They represent full-field kinematic observations without repeating the subset-correlation details already covered in Tutorial 16.

Forces are obtained by averaging the stress response and adding measurement-like noise. The inverse problem therefore receives both kinematic and global mechanical information.

The useful question is not only whether the DIC field looks smooth, but how much DIC and force noise move the identified material parameters.

The chapter should be read together with the code. Every quantity mentioned here is created explicitly in `multimodal_benchmark.py` and regenerated by `reproduce.py`. The purpose is not to hide complexity behind a black box, but to show which assumptions enter the pipeline.

For interpretation, always ask four questions: what is the hidden truth, what is observed, what is reconstructed, and which metric compares them? This four-part logic prevents a visually appealing result from being mistaken for a verified mechanical conclusion.

## Implementation details

Each load case has exact strain fields and noisy DIC-like strain fields. The exact fields are used to generate the true stress response. The noisy fields are used in the inverse problem, mimicking the fact that experiments observe kinematics with error. The force vector is also perturbed, which lets the benchmark represent both kinematic and load-measurement uncertainty.

## What to check while reading

Open `mechanics_and_dic.png` and compare the exact strain field with the DIC error field. The error is small compared with the strain magnitude, but it still enters the inverse system. Then inspect `load_cases.csv`; different loading modes excite different combinations of matrix and fiber terms.

## Typical misunderstanding

Smooth DIC fields are not automatically unbiased DIC fields. A systematic small error can be more damaging than random-looking noise because it can consistently move the parameter estimate. This tutorial makes that effect easy to test by changing `dic_noise` and `force_noise`.
