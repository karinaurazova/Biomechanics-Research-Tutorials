# Why multimodal verification matters

Image-informed biomechanics is not one algorithm but a chain of transformations. A hidden tissue architecture is observed, segmented, converted into descriptors, inserted into a mechanical law, combined with DIC fields and used in an inverse problem. The tutorial makes every link of that chain measurable.

The synthetic strategy is strict: the microstructure is generated first and treated as truth. SEM-like, polarization-like, fluorescence-like and DIC-like observations are rendered from that same truth. Because the hidden answer is known, errors can be assigned to a stage rather than guessed.

Verification and validation are separated. This module verifies whether the computational workflow can recover known masks, orientations, forces and parameters under controlled conditions. It does not claim that the generated images fully reproduce a real microscope or a clinical experiment.

The chapter should be read together with the code. Every quantity mentioned here is created explicitly in `multimodal_benchmark.py` and regenerated by `reproduce.py`. The purpose is not to hide complexity behind a black box, but to show which assumptions enter the pipeline.

For interpretation, always ask four questions: what is the hidden truth, what is observed, what is reconstructed, and which metric compares them? This four-part logic prevents a visually appealing result from being mistaken for a verified mechanical conclusion.

## Implementation details

In the code, the benchmark is deliberately split into small functions instead of one large script. `generate_ground_truth_microstructure` creates the hidden answer. `render_modalities` creates observations. `segment_modalities` produces masks. `recover_structure` estimates orientation and coherence. `build_load_cases` produces strain fields and forces. `parameter_recovery_benchmark` closes the loop by identifying material parameters from different structural inputs. This separation is not only software style; it is the scientific structure of the verification problem.

## What to check while reading

When you inspect a figure or CSV file, do not ask only whether the output is plausible. Ask whether it is close to the known hidden field. A real experiment rarely lets you do this, which is exactly why synthetic verification is useful. If the synthetic benchmark fails, the same weakness would be hidden in real data. If it succeeds, you still need real validation later, but at least the computational chain has passed a controlled test.

## Typical misunderstanding

The most common mistake is to treat multimodal data as automatically better. More modalities can improve robustness, but they can also introduce inconsistent biases. Fusion is useful only when the information added by one modality helps a downstream task. In this tutorial, that downstream task is mechanical parameter recovery, not visual image quality.
