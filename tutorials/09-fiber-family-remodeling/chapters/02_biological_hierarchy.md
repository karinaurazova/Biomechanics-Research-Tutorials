# 2. Biological hierarchy of collagen remodeling

Collagen architecture is organized across molecular, fibrillar, fiber-bundle, tissue, and organ scales. A continuum internal variable is therefore a coarse-grained representation, not a literal biological object.

At the cellular level, fibroblasts, myofibroblasts, smooth-muscle cells, valvular interstitial cells, and other matrix-producing cells sense deformation, matrix stiffness, ligand organization, fluid-induced signals, and biochemical mediators. They can alter synthesis, degradation, contraction, and matrix organization. At the fibrillar level, remodeling may involve lateral growth, enzymatic cleavage, cross-link maturation, sliding, rotation, and changes in crimp. At the tissue level, these events appear as changes in mass fraction, orientation distribution, recruitment, anisotropy, residual stress, and stiffness.

A useful modelling discipline is to distinguish:

- **mechanical cue**: stress, strain, stretch, energy, or a principal direction;
- **sensing rule**: how the cue is filtered, averaged, delayed, and normalized;
- **cellular response**: production, degradation, traction, or phenotypic change;
- **matrix state**: orientation, dispersion, mass, natural stretch, recruitment, or cross-link state;
- **mechanical consequence**: altered stress redistribution and a new cue.

The last step closes the mechanobiological feedback loop. A model that prescribes an orientation history without feeding it back into mechanics is a kinematic remodeling model. A model in which architecture changes the constitutive response and therefore the next stimulus is a coupled mechanobiological model.
