# 11 Differential growth and bending

Differential growth across a thickness is one of the simplest mechanisms by which a flat body acquires curvature. One side prefers a larger natural length than the other. A compatible strip cannot realize both natural lengths while remaining flat.

The reduced model assumes a plane-section total axial stretch

\[
\lambda(z)=\lambda_0+\kappa z,
\]

and a prescribed natural growth stretch \(g(z)\). The elastic stretch is

\[
\lambda_e(z)=\frac{\lambda(z)}{g(z)}.
\]

The coefficients \(\lambda_0\) and \(\kappa\) minimize a quadratic morphoelastic energy. This produces force- and moment-free generalized resultants while leaving a self-equilibrated residual stress distribution.

Uniform growth gives \(\kappa=0\). A linear growth gradient gives nonzero curvature whose magnitude increases with the gradient. The model demonstrates the mechanism clearly but remains reduced: it omits transverse shear, geometric nonlinearities of a full beam or shell, material anisotropy, and three-dimensional boundary effects.

This experiment forms a bridge to morphogenesis and instability. At larger growth mismatches, bending may no longer be the only response; wrinkling, folding, or buckling can emerge. Those phenomena require stability analysis and spatial equilibrium.
