# 06 Free, constrained, and residual stress

If a homogeneous body is allowed to realize exactly its local growth tensor,

\[
\mathbf F=\mathbf F_g,
\qquad
\mathbf F_e=\mathbf I,
\]

and the elastic stress vanishes for a stress-free constitutive reference. This is **free growth**.

If the same growth occurs while the total deformation is fixed, for example \(\mathbf F=\mathbf I\), then

\[
\mathbf F_e=\mathbf F_g^{-1}.
\]

The body is elastically compressed when its preferred natural dimensions increase but its observed dimensions cannot. It stores energy even though no external deformation is imposed after the growth event.

Residual stress does not require an external load. It can arise because a spatially varying \(\mathbf F_g\) cannot be assembled into a compatible body. The current configuration then balances internal elastic distortions subject to equilibrium and boundary conditions.

The tutorial deliberately separates three cases:

- homogeneous free growth: compatible and stress-free;
- homogeneous constrained growth: compatible total placement but externally restricted;
- differential growth: locally stress-free states are mutually incompatible.

These mechanisms should not be conflated. A ring-opening experiment, for example, probes residual stress but does not uniquely reveal the underlying growth tensor. Tutorial 12 will treat ring opening explicitly.
