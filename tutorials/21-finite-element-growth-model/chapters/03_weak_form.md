# Weak form of the growth problem


The strong form of static equilibrium is div sigma + b = 0 in the body, with boundary conditions on displacement and traction.  For the tutorial we set body force to zero and focus on stress generated by growth.  The weak form is obtained by multiplying equilibrium by a virtual displacement and integrating over the body.

After integration by parts, the internal virtual work contains the stress contracted with the virtual strain.  In the small-strain growth model the stress is C : (epsilon(u) - epsilon_g).  Therefore the weak form asks for a displacement u such that the elastic stress performs the same virtual work as the external loads for every admissible virtual displacement.

When there is no external traction, growth still appears in the right-hand side after discretization.  This is sometimes confusing for students.  The growth strain is not an external force in a physical sense.  It becomes an equivalent nodal load because the finite-element equations are written in terms of the unknown displacement field.  The algebraic system has the familiar form K u = f_g.

The stiffness matrix K depends on the elastic material, geometry and shape functions.  The growth load f_g depends on the growth strain field and the same material matrix.  If the growth strain is spatially uniform and the body is completely free, the solution can often accommodate it with little or no stress.  If the growth is incompatible or constraints prevent free expansion, elastic stress remains.

The weak form is also the place where verification starts.  A correct displacement solution should make the residual small on the free degrees of freedom.  Reactions may remain on constrained degrees of freedom.  Large residuals on free degrees of freedom indicate an assembly, boundary condition or linear solve error.
