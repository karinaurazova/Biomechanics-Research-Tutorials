# Collagen maturation and fibrillogenesis

Newly synthesized collagen does not become a mechanically mature fiber instantaneously. Fibrillogenesis includes extracellular processing, nucleation, fibril growth, bundling, and interaction with other collagen types and matrix molecules.

The tutorial distinguishes immature and mature pools. This permits transient states in which total collagen changes little while the mature fraction and stiffness change substantially. It also allows inflammation to remove immature material preferentially or interrupt maturation.

Collagen I and III can be represented as separate components with different stiffnesses and timescales. The current implementation is deliberately coarse: it does not resolve molecular triple helices or fibril diameter distributions.

For interpretation, mature mass should be viewed as a load-bearing fraction, not as a direct measurement of a specific biochemical marker. Mapping model states to assays requires a separate observation model.

## Key modeling checkpoint

State explicitly which quantities are conserved, which are constitutive assumptions, and which are experimentally observable.
