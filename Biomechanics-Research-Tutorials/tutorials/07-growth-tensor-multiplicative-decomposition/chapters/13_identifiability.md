# 13 Identifiability and interpretation limits

The observed total deformation does not uniquely determine the split. For any admissible candidate \(\mathbf F_g\), one can define

\[
\mathbf F_e=\mathbf F\mathbf F_g^{-1}.
\]

Different candidates produce different elastic stresses and energies while reproducing the same \(\mathbf F\). Additional information is therefore required: unloaded geometry, residual-stress measurements, serial imaging, constituent turnover, deposition stretches, material directions, or assumptions about the growth law.

Parameter non-identifiability also arises in homogeneous relaxation. Growth rate, target stress, response saturation, and material stiffness can compensate for each other over a limited observation interval. Fitting one stress trajectory is not enough to identify all of them.

The growth tensor is not synonymous with mass density. The relation between \(J_g\), mass supply, density, and composition depends on the open-system balance laws. A model that sets \(J_g\) equal to a mass ratio implicitly assumes how density changes.

Finally, growth and remodeling are not identical. Multiplicative growth changes a local natural configuration. Remodeling may alter stiffness, anisotropy, fiber orientation, turnover, or deposition history without the same kinematic representation. Later tutorials will compare these mechanisms rather than collapsing them into one tensor.

Results from this tutorial should be reported as verification-ready synthetic demonstrations. They support methodological understanding and code testing, not claims about a specific biological tissue.
