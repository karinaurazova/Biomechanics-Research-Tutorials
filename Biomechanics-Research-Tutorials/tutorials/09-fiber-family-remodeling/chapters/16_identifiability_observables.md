# 16. Identifiability and experimental observables

A single uniaxial curve can often be reproduced by compensating changes in mean orientation, dispersion, fiber mass, stiffness, slack stretch, or deposition prestretch. The tutorial demonstrates a valley of low fitting error in the angle–concentration plane after optimizing mass.

Improved identifiability requires multiple independent observables, for example:

- biaxial mechanical tests;
- shear tests in several material planes;
- unloaded and loaded orientation maps;
- collagen mass fraction or biochemical content;
- crimp or recruitment measurements;
- time-resolved turnover markers;
- residual-stress release experiments;
- layer-specific or spatially resolved data.

Orientation images measured under load must be interpreted carefully: apparent angle changes may combine material rotation, fiber stretch, affine deformation, non-affine reorganization, and biological remodeling. A model intended for remodeling should separate instantaneous kinematics from long-term evolution.

Parameter estimation should report practical identifiability, parameter correlations, uncertainty, and whether the same data were used for calibration and validation.
