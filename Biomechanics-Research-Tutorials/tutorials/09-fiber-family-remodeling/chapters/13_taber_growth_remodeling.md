# 13. Taber's growth-and-remodeling perspective

Taber's work emphasizes that adaptation of living tissues couples mechanics, geometry, material properties, and biological regulation. His aortic model distinguishes fluid shear and fiber stresses as stimuli for different adaptive responses.

For fiber-family remodeling, this perspective suggests several modelling rules:

1. one stimulus need not regulate every internal variable;
2. geometry, mass, orientation, and active tone can adapt on different time scales;
3. a homeostatic target may be component-specific;
4. local adaptation changes the global mechanics and therefore the next stimulus;
5. boundary conditions and hemodynamic control determine whether a feedback law stabilizes or destabilizes the system.

A fiber angle should therefore not be evolved in isolation from mass, prestretch, and loading. The tutorial uses simple examples to show how a loading switch changes orientation history, how family-specific tension redistributes mass, and how a model can reach the same endpoint through distinct internal histories.
