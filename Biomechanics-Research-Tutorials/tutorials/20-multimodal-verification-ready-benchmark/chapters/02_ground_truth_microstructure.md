# Ground-truth microstructure

The benchmark begins with a mathematical microstructure rather than an image. It contains a tissue mask, fiber mask, pores, axial orientation, concentration, fiber fraction, connectivity and true material parameters.

Pores are represented separately because they affect mechanics. Filling a pore may improve a simple fiber mask visually while destroying pore-size information and load-transfer paths.

The orientation field is axial: theta and theta plus pi represent the same fiber axis. This is why all angular errors use an axial difference rather than ordinary angle subtraction.

The chapter should be read together with the code. Every quantity mentioned here is created explicitly in `multimodal_benchmark.py` and regenerated by `reproduce.py`. The purpose is not to hide complexity behind a black box, but to show which assumptions enter the pipeline.

For interpretation, always ask four questions: what is the hidden truth, what is observed, what is reconstructed, and which metric compares them? This four-part logic prevents a visually appealing result from being mistaken for a verified mechanical conclusion.

## Implementation details

The ground-truth object is stored as a dataclass so that every hidden field travels together. The tissue mask defines where the specimen exists. The fiber mask defines the solid fibrous phase. The pore mask is kept separately because pore preservation can be important for permeability, local stress concentration and effective stiffness. The fields `rho_f` and `connectivity` are smoothed descriptors derived from the binary fiber phase; they represent the kind of mesoscale quantities that would often enter a constitutive model.

## What to check while reading

Open `ground_truth_fields.png` and compare the binary fiber mask with the smooth density and connectivity maps. They are related but not identical. This distinction is central: mechanics usually does not react to a single isolated pixel, but it can react strongly to loss of connected pathways or systematic density bias. Also inspect the pore field. A segmentation method that fills pores may look smoother, but it changes a known structural feature.

## Typical misunderstanding

A microstructure generator should not be judged only by visual realism. For verification, controllability is more important. The structure must contain enough complexity to challenge the pipeline, but the hidden fields must remain accessible. That is why this tutorial uses a synthetic pattern with known orientation, concentration and material parameters rather than a purely photographic texture.
