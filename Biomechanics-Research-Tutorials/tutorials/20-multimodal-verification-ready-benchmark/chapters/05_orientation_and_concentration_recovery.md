# Orientation and concentration recovery

Orientation is recovered by two routes: a structure tensor from grayscale images and a polarization-like angle map. The fusion estimate combines axial orientations with doubled-angle averaging.

Doubled-angle averaging is necessary because fibers are axes, not arrows. Averaging cos(2 theta) and sin(2 theta) avoids wrap-around artifacts near 0 and 180 degrees.

The recovered orientation is evaluated where it matters mechanically. A beautiful orientation map is not enough if it is wrong in dense, load-bearing regions.

The chapter should be read together with the code. Every quantity mentioned here is created explicitly in `multimodal_benchmark.py` and regenerated by `reproduce.py`. The purpose is not to hide complexity behind a black box, but to show which assumptions enter the pipeline.

For interpretation, always ask four questions: what is the hidden truth, what is observed, what is reconstructed, and which metric compares them? This four-part logic prevents a visually appealing result from being mistaken for a verified mechanical conclusion.

## Implementation details

The structure-tensor estimate uses local image gradients. Since gradients are strongest across ridges, the ridge direction is obtained by rotating the dominant gradient direction. The polarization estimate directly uses the noisy polarization angle. The fusion estimate combines both using axial doubled-angle averaging, which respects the fact that fiber directions are axes.

## What to check while reading

Open `orientation_recovery.png` and inspect both the recovered angle and the error map. The mean error is useful, but the spatial distribution is more important. Errors in low-density regions may not matter much mechanically; errors inside dense connected fiber regions can strongly bias the anisotropic contribution to stress.

## Typical misunderstanding

Orientation recovery is not just a visualization task. In a constitutive model, orientation determines how strain is projected onto fibers and how stress is rotated back to global coordinates. Therefore a few degrees of error can matter differently depending on loading mode and local fiber density.
