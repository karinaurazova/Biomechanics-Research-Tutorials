# Instructor notes

## Central misconceptions

1. **Concentration and dispersion are synonyms.** Higher concentration means lower dispersion in this parameterization.
2. **A fiber distribution is directional.** The distribution is axial and π-periodic.
3. **The mean angle determines the mechanical response.** The spread and higher-order shape also matter.
4. **Dispersion always softens a material.** Its effect depends on the mean orientation relative to loading.
5. **The orientation tensor contains the full distribution.** It retains only second-order information.
6. **β and GOH κ can be compared numerically.** Their definitions and ranges differ.
7. **A fitted mechanical curve identifies collagen architecture.** Several parameter combinations may be mechanically similar.

## Recommended live-coding sequence

1. Draw two distributions with the same mean and different spread.
2. Introduce the doubled-angle axial density.
3. Verify normalization numerically.
4. Compute $S=I_1/I_0$ and the orientation tensor.
5. Compare analytical and numerical tensors.
6. Introduce the fiber stretch $I_4(\theta)$.
7. Plot which angles are in tension at a chosen stretch.
8. Perform angular integration of fiber energy.
9. Compare aligned and oblique families.
10. Finish with identifiability and validation boundaries.

## Formative assessment checkpoints

- Minute 20: identify the most dispersed distribution without using β values.
- Minute 40: predict the tensor for β = 0.
- Minute 65: identify fibers in tension for horizontal extension.
- Minute 90: predict curve ordering for aligned loading.
- Minute 110: explain why curve ordering can change for an oblique mean.

## Optional advanced discussion

Ask learners to construct two distinct distributions with approximately the same second-order tensor. Discuss why a nonlinear angular integral can distinguish them even when $\mathbf{A}$ cannot.
