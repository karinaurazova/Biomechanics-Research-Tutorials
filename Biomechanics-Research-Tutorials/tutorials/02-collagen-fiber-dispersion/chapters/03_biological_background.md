# 3. Biological and mechanical background

## 3.1 Collagen architecture is distributed

Collagen organization is commonly described by more than one angle. A tissue region may have a preferred direction, a finite angular spread, two symmetric families, or a non-symmetric distribution. Structural constitutive models were developed precisely because these architectural features affect load transfer.

Lanir's structural framework represented tissue response through contributions of microstructural components. Sacks later incorporated experimentally measured planar fiber orientation into a structural model. Gasser, Ogden, and Holzapfel introduced a continuum structure parameter for dispersed arterial collagen, demonstrating that dispersion can substantially change predicted mechanics.

## 3.2 Mean direction is not enough

Consider two regions with the same mean direction of 45 degrees:

- Region A contains a narrow family close to 45 degrees.
- Region B contains fibers spread from approximately 0 to 90 degrees.

Under horizontal tension, Region B includes more fibers close to the loading direction even though both regions have the same mean. Their macroscopic responses can therefore differ.

## 3.3 Sources of dispersion

A measured angular spread may reflect:

- biological organization of fiber bundles;
- spatial variation within the image window;
- crimp and out-of-plane projection;
- remodeling, deposition, and degradation;
- sectioning and imaging artifacts;
- orientation-estimation uncertainty.

The fitted distribution is therefore a model of measured orientation information, not automatically a direct measure of a single biological mechanism.

## 3.4 Distribution versus remodeling

This tutorial studies a fixed orientation distribution and its mechanical consequence. Tutorial 01 studied evolution of a mean direction. A later module can couple both ideas by evolving the mean angle and concentration in time, similar in spirit to remodeling formulations that track angular collagen distributions.
