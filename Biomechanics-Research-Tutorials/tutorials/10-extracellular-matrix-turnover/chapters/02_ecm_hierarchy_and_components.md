# ECM hierarchy and constituent-specific roles

The extracellular matrix is a hierarchy rather than a single material. Fibrillar collagens provide tensile load bearing and recruitment; elastin supports low-strain recoil and usually turns over slowly in adult arteries; proteoglycans and glycosaminoglycans regulate hydration, swelling, transport, and interfibrillar interactions; basement-membrane and matricellular proteins regulate adhesion and signaling.

At the collagen level it is useful to distinguish intracellular synthesis, procollagen secretion, cleavage, fibril nucleation, lateral growth, maturation, enzymatic cross-linking, and eventual proteolytic removal. A model that represents all collagen as one instantly mature pool cannot reproduce delays between transcriptional activation, mass deposition, and changes in stiffness.

Tutorial 10 therefore uses both a reduced maturation cascade and a multicomponent homogenized model. The first resolves precursor, immature collagen, mature collagen, cross-links, MMP, and TIMP. The second compares components with different half-lives, stiffnesses, and mechanosensitivities.

The hierarchy also motivates a strict distinction between composition and mechanics: equal collagen mass does not imply equal fiber diameter, orientation, crimp, cross-link density, prestretch, or tangent modulus.
