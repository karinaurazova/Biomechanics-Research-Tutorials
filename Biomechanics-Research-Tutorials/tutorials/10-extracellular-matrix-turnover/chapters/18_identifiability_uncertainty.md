# Identifiability, parameter compensation, and uncertainty

At steady state, the equation $m=p/k$ admits infinitely many production–removal pairs with the same mass. This creates a ridge of non-identifiability. Faster balanced turnover and slower balanced turnover are indistinguishable from a single mass measurement.

Additional compensation occurs among mature mass, cross-link fraction, stiffness parameter, and deposition stretch. A mechanical curve alone may not uniquely determine these quantities.

Tutorial 10 therefore treats identifiability as a design problem: which perturbations and observations break each compensation? Pulse–chase data constrain survival, biochemical assays constrain enzymes and cross-links, and multiple mechanical loading modes constrain constitutive parameters.

Parameter uncertainty should be propagated to predictions. The current tutorial visualizes deterministic surfaces; Tutorial 22 will address formal uncertainty and sensitivity analysis.

State explicitly which quantities are conserved, which are constitutive assumptions, and which are experimentally observable.
